Overview

Problem & Engineering Context

Commercial autonomous cleaning robots operate in demanding, unpredictable indoor environments — corridors, open-plan offices, retail floors — and must sustain continuous autonomous operation while remaining cost-efficient enough for commercial deployment. Every mechanical sub-system is a direct trade-off between durability, repairability, weight, and manufacturing cost.

At Agora Robotics, the engineering brief was clear: design, prototype, and validate the complete mechanical architecture of an autonomous indoor cleaning robot, bringing sub-systems from concept through to production-ready documentation and physical prototypes. This meant full parametric CAD models, GD&T technical drawings, engineering calculations, and hands-on fabrication of every component.

All fabrication was performed in-house using the company's rapid-prototyping facilities — with additional custom tooling built from scratch to unlock manufacturing processes not previously available.

Mechanical Design

Sub-System Architecture

CH
Chassis & Structure
Structural frame optimised for internal access and modularity. Parametric Fusion 360 assembly with full GD&T documentation. Designed for assembly-line repeatability and serviceability in the field.
DR
Drive & Locomotion
Differential drive kinematics — dynamic stability analysis and wheel torque calculations performed for worst-case terrain transitions. Motor selection validated against load requirements using strength-of-materials methods.
CL
Cleaning Head
Brush roller integration with adjustable contact pressure. Side brush geometry designed for edge-of-wall reach. Suction intake duct dimensioned for target airflow velocity; all geometry constrained in Fusion 360 for iterative optimisation.
SH
Housings & Shell
External shell panels thermoformed in-house on a custom 600×900 mm vacuum-forming table. Internal electronics enclosures and cable management brackets 3D-printed in PLA+ and ABS. All parts with GD&T toleranced for repeatable fit.

All sub-systems were modelled as parametric assemblies in Fusion 360, with interference checks, mass properties analysis, and full tolerance stack-up performed at the assembly level. AutoCAD was used for 2D technical drawing packages, including GD&T callouts, thread specifications, surface finish requirements, and BOM tables for supplier procurement.

Engineering calculations covered strength of materials (structural members under dynamic loading), kinematics (turning radius, differential drive equations), and dynamics (motor torque requirements, acceleration profiles) — ensuring each design decision was analytically validated before committing to prototyping.

Fabrication

Rapid-Prototyping Stack

01
Parametric CAD — Fusion 360 & AutoCAD
Full system modelled as a parametric assembly in Fusion 360 — enabling rapid design iterations by modifying master parameters. Production drawings exported to AutoCAD for GD&T annotation, thread and surface-finish callouts, and complete BOM packages for suppliers.
02
FDM Prototyping — Prusa MK4 & Anycubic Kobra
Functional structural parts, motor mounts, cable management brackets and assembly jigs printed in PLA+ and ABS. FDM used for rapid iteration of geometry and fit — parts could be revised and re-printed within hours of a design change, dramatically shortening the validation cycle.
03
SLA / Resin — Anycubic Photon Mono X
High-resolution resin printing for components requiring fine surface detail, precise mating surfaces, or optical clarity. Used for sensor housings, interface rings, and cosmetic prototype parts where FDM layer lines were unacceptable. Resin parts post-cured under UV and mechanically post-processed where required.
04
CO₂ Laser Cutting — 80 W
Flat-pattern sheet parts, electronics chassis panels, and structural brackets cut directly from DXF output of AutoCAD drawings. Acrylic and thin-gauge steel panels cut for enclosures and internal mounting plates. Laser-cut parts cleaned, deburred, and tapped to drawing specification.
05
Silicone Casting — Gravity & Injection Moulding
Custom two-part silicone molds designed in Fusion 360 and fabricated in-house. Gravity casting used for larger soft-touch interface pads and sealing gaskets. Injection casting with a hand-press applied for smaller, high-detail parts requiring consistent shore hardness and repeatable dimensional accuracy across batches.
06
Vacuum Forming — Custom 600×900 mm Table
Designed and built a 600×900 mm vacuum-forming table from scratch specifically to enable in-house thermoforming of the robot's ABS shell panels. Quartz heater array provides uniform heating of the plastic sheet. A vacuum pump draws air through a perforated platen to form the heated sheet over the mold. This custom tooling eliminated outsourcing costs and enabled rapid shell iteration.

The project delivered a production-ready mechanical system across all major sub-systems of the autonomous cleaning robot — chassis, drive, cleaning head, and external shell. Bringing the vacuum-forming capability in-house was a significant manufacturing achievement: it removed a major outsourcing dependency, cut unit tooling cost, and reduced lead time for shell iterations from weeks to hours.

All design decisions were backed by first-principles engineering calculations and validated through physical prototypes before being committed to production documentation — ensuring the final design is both analytically sound and practically manufacturable.
