Overview

The Sweep Platform

Sweep is a commercial autonomous indoor floor-cleaning robot — a mobile platform combining wet scrubbing, vacuum recovery, autonomous navigation and self-managed docking for continuous unattended operation in retail, warehousing, office and residential spaces. Field deployments to date include Orange HQ, IKEA, Dedeman Central Warehouse, Stejari Residence and Agora's own test sites.

The engineering brief was to design, prototype, and validate the complete mechanical architecture of both the robot and its docking station, evolving the platform from concept-stage hardware through to serial-production-ready systems. Ownership covered every load-bearing structure, every water-management element, the entire cleaning subsystem, all custom silicone components, and the full technical documentation pack for each manufactured part.

The platform has progressed through nine hardware generations — Sweep D1 → D8 plus the first small-series production version E1 — each delivering measurable engineering improvements in capacity, robustness, serviceability, manufacturability and unit cost. In parallel, the docking station moved from plywood test rigs to an IPX6-certified metal unit with reliable water loading, detergent dosing and waste evacuation.

Platform Evolution

From D1 to E1 — Nine Hardware Generations

Each Sweep generation targeted a specific failure mode or limitation observed in the previous, validated through customer deployments and lab testing. Volumes, materials and motor architectures have all been re-engineered iteratively.

D1
Sweep D1 — Concept-stage prototype · 10/2023
First fully CAD-developed Sweep. Laser-cut steel + CNC-bent (abkant) frame; in-house TIG-welded aluminium water tanks (driven by supplier deviations on the externally bent parts). 2× Dyson V3 vacuum motors, residual-water valve inspired by wastewater treatment plants — 3D-moulded silicone elements validated to 50,000+ open/close cycles with no measurable degradation. First field tests at Băicoi in January 2024.
D2
Sweep D2 — DC brush motors · 02/2024
Migrated cleaning brushes to DC motors; full redesign of the lower module to fit the new actuators (+35 mm internal height, +50 mm total robot height). Clean-water capacity extended by ≈4 L. Residual-tank wash circuit reworked with copper piping fitted with 0.8 mm 3D-printer nozzles for high-velocity rinse jets.
D3
Sweep D3 — Brushless vacuum + new Lidar · 04/2024
First brushless vacuum-motor implementation. Residual-water valve rebuilt around a linear actuator + machined metal housing, replacing the earlier servo + 3D-printed assembly for far higher service life. New Lidar evaluated in parallel. Public showcase at Automatica Munich 2024.
D4
Sweep D4 — IR-LED docking · 06/2024
First robot integrating an on-board camera + IR-LED beacons on the docking station — an in-house Agora detection method enabling precise station alignment even in zero ambient lighting (a key differentiator). Many 3D-printed structural parts replaced with machined metal equivalents for production durability.
D5
Sweep D5 — Mobile squeegee · 10/2024
First version with a mobile rear squeegee and a structurally redesigned cleaning head (informed by IKEA field tests on D4). Residual-tank wash upgraded to an aluminium pipe with a rotating nozzle for full internal cleaning. After observing soft caster-wheel response at Stejari Residence, the suspension was rebuilt around high-load stamping springs — rolled back into every prior unit.
D6
Sweep D6 — Sonar level sensing · 02/2025
Replaced the unreliable float-based residual-water level sensor (fouled by iron filings and floor films) with a sonar transducer. Following deployment at Orange HQ, added a second Lidar on the top cover and a second front RealSense camera — both later promoted to primary sensors across the entire fleet.
D7
Sweep D7 — Major redesign · 03/2025
Full mechanical overhaul for serviceability and large-debris resistance: 23 L clean / 18 L residual capacity, drain slope on the residual tank, 40 mm waste outlet (larger than the intake) for unrestricted flow. Tanks moved to 1 mm stainless steel (~9700 mm cumulative weld length per robot, TIG-welded in-house). Vacuum motor relocated inside the residual tank → +200% suction performance and reduced noise. Single-pump architecture with electrovalve manifold + 3D-printed two-point spray distributor. Added lateral squeegees, TOF clean-water sensor, foldable lockable electronics tray, manual 20 cm cleaning-head pull-out, rear depth camera for obstacle-detection redundancy.
D8
Sweep D8 — New sensor stack · 05/2025
Sensor refresh — newer cameras, sonars and Lidar with better cost/performance ratios, plus a more stable industrial PC. Started migrating mechanical production to the Băicoi factory and outsourcing metal machining. Dedeman warehouse trials identified failure modes in the extensible cable system and water droplets on the TOF lens; redundancy added with sonar + a glued-on bottom-tank ultrasonic sensor running in parallel.
E1
Sweep E1 — Small-series production · 09/2025
First version intended for serial assembly at the Băicoi factory. Tanks moved to 1.5 mm sheet (supplier requirement); residual-tank usable volume raised to 80% of total height with a deliberate safety headspace. Top cover now opens via two levers replacing 24 screws, with a redesigned pivot-and-translate hinge and 150 N gas struts — and zero shear points. Robot height +80 mm for serviceability, drain slope 2° → 9°, length +15 mm to fit protective brushes around the caster, new front safety bumper, redesigned front brush that ejects large debris instead of jamming. Design optimisation: 149 → 55 line items received at Agora before factory assembly.
Mechanical Design

Sub-System Architecture

CH
Chassis & Stainless-Steel Tanks
Sheet-metal chassis from laser-cut, CNC-bent steel. Clean and residual water tanks fabricated from 1 mm (D7) → 1.5 mm (E1) stainless steel, TIG-welded with ~9700 mm cumulative weld length per robot. Designed for alkaline-detergent compatibility, internal access for service, and active drain-slope control.
DR
Drive & Caster Suspension
Differential-drive base with rear caster. After Stejari Residence trials, the caster suspension was rebuilt around high-load stamping springs to absorb floor irregularities. Wheel torque and stability analytics validated against worst-case payload (full tanks + battery).
CU
Cleaning Unit
Rotating front brush + rear mobile squeegee + lateral squeegees for full edge-of-wall coverage during 180° turns. E1 front brush redesigned to push large debris outward (anti-jamming). Cleaning head can be pulled out 20 cm by hand for in-field maintenance while remaining electrically connected.
WM
Water Management
Clean → spray → recovery loop using a single pump and an electrovalve manifold (D7). Custom 3D-printed two-point distributor for uniform floor spray. Residual line: 40 mm evacuation port (oversized vs intake), drain-slope tank floor, in-tank rotating nozzle for self-cleaning, TOF + sonar + ultrasonic for triple-redundant level sensing.
VS
Vacuum System
Migrated from 2× Dyson V3 brushed motors (D1) → single brushless motor (D3) → motor relocated inside the residual tank (D7) — a +200% suction-performance step with lower acoustic noise. Internal ducting dimensioned for target airflow velocity in Fusion 360.
NV
Sensors & Navigation
Sensor stack converged from a single Lidar + AprilTag docking (D1–D3) to a dual-Lidar + dual-RealSense + IR-LED-beacon docking architecture (D6+). Rear depth camera added on D7 to close gaps in obstacle detection. All sensor mounts CAD-modelled with serviceability and ingress-protected routing.

Every sub-system was modelled as a parametric Fusion 360 assembly with interference checks, mass properties analysis, and full tolerance stack-up. AutoCAD was used for GD&T technical drawing packages, supplier-ready BOM tables, and DXF outputs for laser cutting and bending. Engineering calculations covered strength of materials, kinematics and dynamics — every design decision analytically backed before fabrication.

Docking Station

From Plywood Rig to IPX6-Certified Unit

The docking station evolved in parallel with the robot — handling autonomous clean-water refill, detergent dosing, and residual-water evacuation. Four major iterations, with full mechanical, hydraulic and electronics integration.

T0
Test rig — Plywood + acrylic · 11/2023
First version assembled from laser-cut plywood and acrylic panels to validate component layout. Followed by two larger metal prototypes to ease hydraulic and electrical integration. ~15–18 iterations of 3D-printed waste-water connectors developed in parallel before locking the final geometry.
V1
V1 — First metal station · 03/2024
Laser-cut + bent + in-house TIG-welded metal structure. Linear actuator driving the clean-water loading interface. Detergent reservoir + dosing system. ~10 connector iterations between station and robot for residual-water evacuation. Station-robot pose detection via AprilTag.
V2
V2 — IPX6 certification · 07/2024
Sealing, gasketing and cable routing redesigned around the Ingress Protection IPX6 standard — protection against powerful water jets from any direction. First commercially-certified Sweep dock.
V3
V3 — Detergent drawer · 09/2024
Dedicated 2-slot drawer for detergent canisters, with automatic recognition of canister presence and type, plus continuous monitoring of dispensed vs. remaining detergent volume.
VF
Final version · from 09/2024
Consolidated all prior improvements + the D4 IR-LED docking system. Reliable water loading, detergent dosing and waste evacuation in a single IPX6-certified package.

Fabrication

Rapid-Prototyping Stack

01
Parametric CAD — Fusion 360 + AutoCAD
Every sub-assembly maintained as a parametric Fusion 360 model — design intent captured as master parameters so generation-to-generation changes propagate cleanly. AutoCAD for GD&T drawings, supplier BOMs, and DXF/PDF release packages.
02
FDM Printing
Functional structural parts, motor mounts, cable brackets, fluid distributors and assembly jigs in PLA+ / ABS / PETG. Print-fit-revise loop closed within hours of a design change.
03
SLA / Resin Printing
High-resolution resin for sensor housings, fine interface geometry, and surface-finish-critical components. Master patterns also printed for silicone moulds.
04
CO₂ Laser Cutting — 80 W
Personally operated cutter — acrylic, plexiglass, thin-gauge steel and wood panels cut to ±0.1 mm from AutoCAD DXFs. Material selection and machine calibration owned in-house.
05
Silicone Casting — Gravity & Injection
Designed and cast silicone squeegees, gaskets, hoses and seals using in-house two-part moulds. Hand-press injection delivered repeatable Shore-A hardness and dimensional accuracy — replacing supplier quotes at a cost reduction of more than 5×.
06
Vacuum Forming + TIG Welding
Custom 600×900 mm vacuum-forming table designed and built from scratch for in-house thermoforming of ABS shell panels. Stainless-steel tanks TIG-welded by the Agora team — ~9700 mm of weld per D7 robot, integrated directly with the cut/bent steel chassis.
07
External Manufacturing & Supplier Coordination
CNC milling / turning and bent-metal sub-assemblies sourced externally with full DFM packages, drawings, and inspection criteria. From the E series onward, mechanical production migrated to the Băicoi factory — driving an explicit redesign for serial assembly.

Outcomes

Impact & Deliverables

Across two years of platform development, the project delivered a production-ready Sweep robot and IPX6 docking station, validated through field deployments at Orange HQ, IKEA, Dedeman Central Warehouse, Stejari Residence and Agora's own sites — with the platform now transitioning to small-series assembly at the Băicoi factory.

Beyond the hardware itself, the work established a full in-house rapid-prototyping capability at Agora Robotics — combining FDM, SLA, CO₂ laser cutting, silicone casting, vacuum forming and TIG welding — which collapsed the design-to-validation cycle from days to hours and unlocked unit-cost reductions (>5× on silicone parts) that would otherwise have required outsourcing.

Every design decision was backed by first-principles engineering — strength of materials, kinematics, dynamics, tolerance stacks — and validated on real robots in real environments before being committed to serial-production documentation.
